Agentic LLMOps

Can you drop a lightweight critic and fixer layer into a multi-agent code generation pipeline and get better results without retraining anything? Ten phases of experiments across five models turned that into a precise, defensible law, complete with confidence intervals and an ablation that says exactly which part does the work.

The architecture

Three agents and a do-no-harm gate

A Planner writes a solution. A Critic scores it for hallucination risk between 0 and 1. If that score crosses a threshold the Fixer rewrites the code, and a Post-Critic scores the result. A reversion gate then checks whether the rewrite actually improved the score and discards it if it did not. Everything runs locally through Ollama. No API calls, no fine-tuning, fully reproducible.

A baseline of Planner straight to Post-Critic runs alongside every condition, so each result is a clean before and after. The operating threshold τ settles at 0.70.

The central law

The weaker the model, the more monitoring helps

Same pipeline, same critic and fixer, five different Planner models. The weak coding model gained more than 30 points. The strong ones lost a little. The relationship was monotonic and held across every phase and every threshold tested. I call it the Inverse Capability Hypothesis.

StarCoder2 is the floor case that proves the rule from below. At roughly zero baseline the fixer has nothing to work with, so the benefit collapses back to nothing. The gain is largest in the middle-low band, where the model is wrong often but not hopeless.

Making it defensible

Error bars on the headline claim

A single deterministic run has zero variance and no confidence interval, and reviewers at a venue like ICSE or FSE will challenge a number with no error bar. So I reran the two endpoints as three genuinely independent trials each, with a non-deterministic planner, and computed 95 percent intervals. Both endpoints exclude zero, which is what makes the law statistically defensible rather than a pair of lucky runs.

What actually does the work

Ablation, one change at a time

Between the net-negative Phase 2 and the net-positive Phase 3, three things changed at once: an 8B fixer, the problem statement handed to the fixer as context, and the reversion gate. I turned each one off in isolation on Code Llama to see which carried the result.

Configurationpass@1Contribution of the missing piece
Baseline, no monitoring20%reference
Full pipeline, all on52%control
Without problem context48%context worth +4pp
With 3B fixer instead of 8B51%model upgrade worth +1pp
Without the reversion gate53%gate costs 1pp here
The surprising part is the gate. On a weak model the gate slightly lowers raw pass@1, because almost any fix is an improvement when you start at 20 percent. Its real value shows up on strong models, where it reverts bad rewrites and stops monitoring from doing harm. Giving the fixer the problem statement was the single biggest lever on the weak model.

Core findings

What ten phases showed

Monitoring benefit is inversely proportional to baseline capability. The central result, now backed by 95 percent intervals that exclude zero at both ends. A model that already gets nine of ten right has little to gain and real risk of harm. A model that gets two of ten right has everything to gain.
The reversion gate is a safety device, not an accelerator. It reverts 73.4 percent of attempted fixes. On weak models that costs a point of raw accuracy, on strong models it is what keeps the pipeline from going negative. Do no harm.
The fixer, not the critic, is the bottleneck. Scaling the critic to 8B did nothing in Phase 2. A bigger judge gives better scores but cannot rescue a fixer that introduces fresh bugs.
Context beats raw model size for the fixer. The ablation says handing the fixer the problem statement mattered more than upgrading its parameter count.

The full arc

Ten phases, from first signal to publishable

Phase 1

Baseline and a calibration trap

3B models throughout. The critic detected risk reliably but the small fixer was net negative. Setting the threshold too low made the fixer fire on 74 percent of problems and dropped accuracy by 18 points. Threshold calibration clearly mattered.

Phase 2

A bigger critic was not the answer

Scaling the critic to 8B while keeping a 3B fixer stayed net negative across all 18 conditions. This pinned the bottleneck on the fixer, not the critic.

Phase 3

The turning point

An 8B fixer, the problem statement as context, and a reversion gate together flipped the direction to a positive +4.85 point average, with a 95 percent CI of +3.36 to +6.34 and p of 0.010. The gate reverted 73.4 percent of fixes.

Phase 4 and 4b

Cross-model comparison

Extended to Code Llama 7B with a full sweep. Code Llama gained 32 points, the largest in the study, and the inverse relationship between capability and benefit became visible.

Phase 5

The pattern holds

Added DeepSeek Coder 6.7B at -1 point and Qwen2.5 Coder 7B at -3 points. The inverse law held consistently across architectures.

Phase 6

Threshold and retry sweep

Swept τ across 0.60, 0.70, 0.75. At 0.70 the system stopped over-triggering with no loss for weak models, and 0.75 was identical, marking the plateau. For Llama 3.1 8B the fixer trigger rate fell from 46 to 10 percent and latency dropped 39 percent. A single temperature retry lifted Code Llama's pass@2 from 20 to 38 percent.

Phase 7

Confidence intervals on the endpoints

Prompted by advisor feedback that single-trial numbers would not survive review. Three independent trials at the two endpoints gave +30 to +31 points for Code Llama and -2 to -4 for Qwen, both with intervals that exclude zero.

Phase 8

Component ablation

One change at a time on Code Llama. Problem context was the biggest single lever at +4 points, the fixer model upgrade added +1, and the gate slightly lowered raw accuracy on a weak model while remaining the safeguard for strong ones.

Phase 9

Extending intervals to all five models, and a methodology catch

Ran the interval study across the remaining models, then caught a real flaw. The planner caches were built deterministically, so the three trials were identical and carried no genuine variance. Worth flagging honestly rather than reporting fake error bars.

Phase 10

Fixing the variance, then cross-benchmark generalization

Regenerated the caches with a non-deterministic planner for true multi-trial intervals, then tested whether the law survives harder benchmarks. HumanEval+ with 164 problems and extra edge-case tests probes whether the fixer mends logic or only syntax, and MBPP tests a different problem distribution. Built to run on GPU through Docker, aimed at an ICSE or FSE submission.

Status

Where it stands

The experiment phases are complete. A conference paper is in progress, targeting a software engineering or LLMOps venue in mid 2026. The work is supervised by Prof. Vahid Alizadeh and Prof. Noriko Tomuro at DePaul University.
