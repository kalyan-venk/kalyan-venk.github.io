The setup

Real human judgment, then adversarial pressure

The data backbone is Anthropic's HH-RLHF dataset, 91,000 pairwise human preference annotations. A rater sees two AI responses to the same conversation and simply picks the one they prefer. No rubric, no scoring guide, only judgment. Unfolding each pair into its chosen and rejected halves gives roughly 182,000 labeled responses to train on.

For the adversarial side I used LLM-Bar, 419 pairs purpose built to fool automated judges. The plan was deliberately simple. Train honest scoring models on the preference data, then see how each one behaves when something is engineered to trip it.

Attack surface

Four ways to fool a judge

LLM-Bar groups its adversarial pairs into four families. Each one targets a different weakness in how automated evaluators decide what looks good.

Neighbor

A worse answer made by small local edits to the better one, so the two look almost identical on the surface. This is the attack that broke Logistic Regression hardest.

GPT instruction

A weaker answer generated by prompting a strong model with the instruction, fluent and confident but not actually better.

GPT output

A polished variation crafted to carry the markers of quality, length, structure, vocabulary, without the substance.

Manual

Hand written deceptive answers, the hardest and most creative cases, designed by people specifically to exploit judge blind spots that automated generation misses.

The headline

Identical on paper, opposite under attack

All three models scored near 0.50 AUC-ROC on clean evaluation, which is the point and not a failure. Surface features do not reliably predict human preference. The real separation only shows up once you attack them.

Same training data. Same clean numbers. More than 75 points apart under pressure. Logistic Regression picked the manipulated worse answer almost four times out of five. XGBoost, built on identical inputs, barely moved. No standard benchmark would ever surface that gap, which is exactly why adversarial testing has to be part of choosing an evaluator.

Unsupervised structure

Length is the opposite of quality

Before touching a single label, I ran three clustering algorithms across all 182,000 responses to see what naturally falls out. K-Means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical Ward linkage all converged on the same four archetypes, which told me the structure was real and not an artifact. Then I checked which archetype humans actually preferred.

The longest responses were the least preferred. I went in assuming elaborate answers would win. 182,000 human judgments said the opposite. A scorer that rewards length is not measuring quality, it is measuring word count, and that is precisely the lever an adversary pulls.

Takeaways

What a team should walk away with

  • Clean benchmark accuracy tells you almost nothing about whether an evaluator can be trusted in an adversarial setting. Two models tied on AUC-ROC differed by over 75 points under attack.
  • Pick your automated judge by stress-testing it, not by reading a leaderboard. Know which attack families break which architecture and by how much.
  • Be suspicious of any quality signal that correlates with length. Across this dataset, longer meant less preferred.
