Can the judge be fooled

Inference-Lens

LLM as judge is the default evaluation paradigm now. Almost nobody is asking how easily that judge can be deceived. So I built an NLP evaluation pipeline to find out. It is a natural language text classification problem: I trained scoring models on 182K real human-labeled responses, then put them under systematic adversarial NLP pressure to see where they break and by how much.

The setup

Honest human judgment on natural language, then adversarial pressure

I started from Anthropic's HH-RLHF dataset, 170,000 plus human preference annotations. A rater sees 2 AI responses to the same conversation and just picks the one they prefer. No rubric, no scoring guide, only judgment. That is the honest NLP signal I trained on. The task is fundamentally one of natural language understanding: can a model learn what humans find genuinely useful or convincing in text?

Then I tried to break them. LLM-Bar gives me 419 adversarially constructed NLP pairs built specifically to fool automated text evaluators. My plan was simple: train NLP scoring models on the clean preference data, then see how each architecture behaves the moment something is engineered to trip it.

3 things I walked away with

The signal was there. It cancelled itself out. Pooled across the dataset, 4 surface features sit at chance: 51.0% and 51.1% accuracy. Split the data by subset and the same features come alive. On helpfulness data, length is the strongest predictor and longer answers win, coefficient +0.220. On harmlessness data it is still the strongest predictor and longer answers lose, coefficient −0.252. Pool the 2 and length predicts nothing, because it predicts opposite things in each half.
Competence is not robustness. DeBERTa was the only model that learned the task, and it degraded the most: 19.3 points, against 18.3 for Logistic Regression and 8.4 for XGBoost. The weak models could not fall far because they were already at chance. Being good at the job is what gives an adversary something to take.
Every model was inverted, not just confused. All 3 architectures scored below 50 percent on adversarial pairs. On a forced binary choice that is systematic inversion: the attacks do not make a judge uncertain, they reliably flip it. Neighbor, the smallest edit of the 4, beat every architecture.

Attack surface

4 ways to fool a judge

LLM-Bar groups its adversarial pairs into 4 families. Each one goes after a different weakness in how automated evaluators decide what looks good.

Neighbor

A worse answer made by small local edits to the better one, so the 2 look almost identical on the surface. This is the attack that broke Logistic Regression hardest.

GPT instruction

A weaker answer generated by prompting a strong model with the instruction. Fluent and confident, but not actually better.

GPT output

A polished variation crafted to carry the markers of quality, length, structure, vocabulary, with none of the substance.

Manual

Hand written deceptive answers. The hardest and most creative cases, built by people specifically to exploit the blind spots automated generation misses.

The headline

2 models, identical on paper, opposite under fire

On the held-out split the 2 feature based judges picked the better response about half the time and DeBERTa cleared them by under 8 points. Then every one of them fell below 50% under attack. The real separation only shows up once you attack them and that is the argument of the whole project.

The model you would pick from the leaderboard is the one that fails hardest. DeBERTa wins on clean data by nearly 8 points of accuracy and loses the most under attack. Rank these 3 by benchmark score and you rank them almost exactly wrong for adversarial robustness. That is why you cannot choose an evaluator without attacking it first.

What the data said

Length is the opposite of quality

Before I touched a single label, I wanted to know what the response space naturally looks like, so I clustered 182K sentence embeddings with K-Means, DBSCAN and Ward. K-Means selected 4 groups by silhouette and those became the archetypes below. Worth being honest about how strong that structure is: the silhouette score is 0.020, close to zero, and allowed to choose for themselves Ward and DBSCAN both prefer 2 groups rather than 4. These are useful descriptive labels, not cleanly separated clusters.

The longest responses were the least preferred. I went in assuming elaborate answers would win. The humans disagreed. A scorer that rewards length is not measuring quality, it is measuring word count and that is exactly the lever an adversary pulls.

Takeaways

What a team should walk away with

  • Clean benchmark accuracy tells you almost nothing about whether an NLP evaluator can be trusted under adversarial attack. The best model on the benchmark degraded the most under it.
  • Pick your automated NLP judge by stress-testing it against adversarial text, not by reading a leaderboard. Know which NLP attack families break which architecture and by how much.
  • Be suspicious of any quality signal that tracks length. It is not that length means nothing, it is that length means opposite things depending on whether the user wanted help or wanted something they should not get. An evaluator leaning on it is silently assuming which situation it is in.
