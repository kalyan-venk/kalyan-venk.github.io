Can the judge be fooled

Inference-Lens

LLM as judge is the default evaluation paradigm now. Almost nobody is asking how easily that judge can be deceived. So I built an NLP evaluation pipeline to find out. It is a natural language text classification problem: I trained scoring models on 170K+ real human preference annotations, then put them under systematic adversarial NLP pressure to see where they break and by how much.

The setup

Honest human judgment on natural language, then adversarial pressure

I started from Anthropic's HH-RLHF dataset, 170,000 plus human preference annotations. A rater sees 2 AI responses to the same conversation and just picks the one they prefer. No rubric, no scoring guide, only judgment. That is the honest NLP signal I trained on. The task is fundamentally one of natural language understanding: can a model learn what humans find genuinely useful or convincing in text?

Then I tried to break them. LLM-Bar gives me 419 adversarially constructed NLP pairs built specifically to fool automated text evaluators. My plan was simple: train NLP scoring models on the clean preference data, then see how each architecture behaves the moment something is engineered to trip it.

3 things I walked away with

Clean accuracy near 0.50 is the point, not the failure. Logistic Regression, XGBoost and a DeBERTa-v3-small fine-tune all landed around 0.50 AUC-ROC on clean evaluation. Surface features like readability and length just do not predict what a human prefers.
Identical on paper, opposite under attack. Under a Neighbor attack, Logistic Regression picked the worse manipulated answer 78.4 percent of the time, almost 4 times in 5. XGBoost, trained on the same data, degraded only 2.4 percent. No clean benchmark would ever show you that gap.
Length is the opposite of quality. 3 clustering algorithms over the full dataset agreed on 4 archetypes. The most verbose cluster had the lowest preference rate at 48.9 percent. The shorter, conversational one had the highest at 51.6 percent.

Attack surface

4 ways to fool a judge

LLM-Bar groups its adversarial pairs into 4 families. Each one goes after a different weakness in how automated evaluators decide what looks good.

Neighbor

A worse answer made by small local edits to the better one, so the 2 look almost identical on the surface. This is the attack that broke Logistic Regression hardest.

GPT instruction

A weaker answer generated by prompting a strong model with the instruction. Fluent and confident, but not actually better.

GPT output

A polished variation crafted to carry the markers of quality, length, structure, vocabulary, with none of the substance.

Manual

Hand written deceptive answers. The hardest and most creative cases, built by people specifically to exploit the blind spots automated generation misses.

The headline

2 models, identical on paper, opposite under fire

All 3 models scored near 0.50 AUC-ROC on clean evaluation. The real separation only shows up once you attack them and that is the argument of the whole project.

Same training data and the same clean scores, yet more than 75 points apart under pressure. If I had only looked at the benchmark, I would have called these 2 interchangeable. They are not. That is exactly why you cannot choose an evaluator without attacking it first.

What the data said

Length is the opposite of quality

Before I touched a single label, I wanted to know what the response space naturally looks like. 3 clustering algorithms over the full dataset all landed on the same 4 archetypes, which told me the structure was real. Then I checked which one humans actually preferred and it surprised me.

The longest responses were the least preferred. I went in assuming elaborate answers would win. The humans disagreed. A scorer that rewards length is not measuring quality, it is measuring word count and that is exactly the lever an adversary pulls.

Takeaways

What a team should walk away with

  • Clean benchmark accuracy tells you almost nothing about whether an NLP evaluator can be trusted under adversarial attack. 2 models tied on AUC-ROC differed by over 75 points the moment I applied NLP perturbation pressure.
  • Pick your automated NLP judge by stress-testing it against adversarial text, not by reading a leaderboard. Know which NLP attack families break which architecture and by how much.
  • Be suspicious of any quality signal that tracks length. Across 170K+ natural language judgments, longer text meant less preferred.
